# Teammate B Findings: Legal Domain Expertise for AI Startup Contract Negotiations

## Overview

This report covers legal domain content for the /legal command: key concepts, contract types,
negotiation frameworks, red flags, and escalation criteria for AI startup founders.

---

## Key Findings

### 1. Contract Review Best Practices for AI/ML Startups

AI startup contracts require more careful review than standard SaaS agreements because:
- AI systems can "learn" from customer data, creating ongoing IP and liability exposure
- Output ownership is often ambiguous and commercially consequential
- Regulatory requirements (EU AI Act, US state AI laws) are evolving rapidly
- Standard boilerplate clauses routinely fail to address AI-specific risks

**Core review discipline:**
1. Identify who owns what: background IP, customer data, generated outputs
2. Trace data flow: what goes in, what comes out, what the vendor retains
3. Check every liability exclusion: "as-is" disclaimers are common and dangerous
4. Map indemnification: who defends whom against what claims
5. Find the exit: termination rights, data deletion, continuity provisions

### 2. Key Legal Concepts Founders Must Understand

#### IP Assignment and Work-for-Hire

**Work-for-hire doctrine** only applies to copyrightable works. For AI/ML work:
- Patent rights, trade secrets, and algorithm ownership require explicit assignment clauses
- "Work made for hire" for independent contractors must be agreed in writing *before* work begins
- Covers only specific enumerated categories of works under US copyright law

**Practical rules:**
- All contractors and employees must sign IP assignment agreements before starting work
- Assignment clause should cover: inventions, discoveries, improvements, works created in scope of engagement
- "Prior inventions" schedule: let contractors list what they're keeping; everything else is yours
- Beware vague language like "related to company business" -- courts interpret this narrowly

**AI-specific IP complications:**
- RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) libraries may contain third-party licensed materials
- Fine-tuned models create layered IP: base model (vendor's), fine-tuning data (yours?), resulting weights (disputed)
- Outputs generated by customized models: customers invariably want to own them, vendors resist
- Copyright Office (May 2025): AI-generated outputs without human creative authorship may not be copyrightable

#### Indemnification and Liability Caps

**Standard structure:**
- General liability cap: typically limited to fees paid in 12 months (inadequate for AI risk)
- Mutual indemnification: each party defends the other against third-party claims arising from their own breach
- Carve-outs from cap: IP infringement, gross negligence, willful misconduct, data breaches

**Negotiation targets:**
- Push cap up to 2-3x annual fees minimum; unlimited for IP and data breach claims
- Require vendor to indemnify for: third-party IP infringement claims, data breaches from vendor systems, regulatory violations caused by vendor's AI
- Resist one-sided indemnification that only protects the vendor
- Watch for mandatory arbitration clauses that eliminate court access -- expensive for startups

**Red flags:**
- Liability capped at subscription fees only (no floor for damages)
- Vendor disclaims all liability for AI output errors, hallucinations, or bias
- Indemnification only flows one way (vendor is indemnified, you are not)

#### Data Rights and AI Training Data Ownership

**Key principle:** Data itself cannot be "owned" under US law. Contracts must specify *usage rights and restrictions* explicitly.

**Three data categories to address in every AI contract:**
1. **Input data**: what you send to the AI system (prompts, documents, datasets)
2. **Output data**: what the AI generates in response
3. **Training/improvement data**: whether vendor can use your inputs/outputs to train their model

**Critical provisions to include:**
- Explicit prohibition: vendor may not use customer data to train, fine-tune, or improve AI models without opt-in consent
- Output ownership: customer owns all outputs generated from customer data and prompts
- License scope: any license granted to vendor is strictly limited to delivering the contracted service
- Retention and deletion: specify timeline, including backups, caches, shadow copies
- Subprocessors: require approval before vendor shares data with third parties

**2025 litigation precedent:** Fastcase v. Alexi Technologies (Nov 2025) -- licensor sued when licensee used licensed data to train commercial AI product. Illustrates why precise license drafting matters for both sides.

**Data partnership structures (AI startups offering data as currency):**
- Data consortium model: customers opt-in to contribute data in exchange for revenue share or improved model performance
- Licensing model: explicit paid license agreements (per OpenAI/AP partnership pattern)
- Each participant retains ownership of their contributions; grants non-exclusive, field-limited license to jointly developed model

#### Non-Compete and Non-Solicitation

**Federal landscape (as of 2026):**
- FTC's non-compete ban rule was blocked by federal courts in 2024; FTC dismissed appeal in Sep 2025
- No federal ban currently in effect; state law governs

**State variation:**
- **Complete bans**: California, North Dakota, Minnesota, Oklahoma, Montana, Wyoming
- **Wage-threshold bans**: CO, DC, IL, ME, MD, MA, NV, NH, OR, RI, VA, WA
- Most states: enforceable if reasonable in scope (12-24 months, limited geography, direct competition only)

**For AI startup founders:**
- Hiring talent from Big Tech: assess whether their non-competes are enforceable in your state
- CA-based employees: non-competes essentially unenforceable; non-solicitation has limits too
- Use NDAs and trade secret protection as the primary defense regardless of jurisdiction
- Non-solicitation (employees and customers) survives in most states where non-competes are banned
- Employee agreements: include IP assignment + NDA + non-solicitation as baseline; non-compete only where enforceable and necessary

#### Representations and Warranties

**What vendors typically offer (and why it's insufficient):**
- "As-is, as-available" -- no guarantee of accuracy, reliability, or fitness
- Explicit disclaimer that AI outputs are not warranted to be correct
- "Independent verification required" language shifting responsibility to deployer

**What to negotiate:**
- Compliance with current and emerging AI regulations (EU AI Act, US state AI laws)
- AI governance framework: vendor conducts risk assessments and maintains audit logs
- Training data provenance: model trained only on permissioned data; vendor indemnifies for IP infringement
- Data security: specific security standards (SOC 2, ISO 27001), breach notification timeline
- Output quality: SLAs for accuracy/performance in specific use cases where objectively measurable

**Representations you should make as an AI startup (selling to customers):**
- You have rights to all training data used in your models
- Your AI complies with applicable laws in customer's jurisdiction
- You have processes to detect and mitigate bias in AI outputs

#### Termination Clauses

**Termination for convenience:** Either party can exit with notice (30-90 days typical). Ensure you have this right.

**Termination for cause:** Triggers should include: material breach not cured within 30 days, insolvency, regulatory enforcement action, data breach.

**What happens at termination:**
- Data return or deletion (specify format, timeline, certification of deletion)
- Transition assistance: vendor must cooperate for N days to enable migration
- License survival: do any licenses survive? Under what terms?
- Payment obligations: no payment for services not yet received; pro-rata refund of prepaid fees

**Key provisions:**
- No automatic renewal without affirmative opt-in (or provide opt-out window: 30-90 days before renewal)
- No unilateral price increases mid-term
- Change-of-control termination right: if vendor is acquired, you can exit

### 3. Common Contract Types for AI Startups

#### SaaS Agreements (as AI vendor or customer)

**Core sections:**
- Scope of services and acceptable use
- Data processing addendum (required for GDPR, CCPA compliance)
- SLAs and remedies for downtime
- IP ownership (background, foreground, outputs)
- Limitation of liability and indemnification
- Subscription terms, pricing, auto-renewal

**2026 trend:** Agentic AI systems require different model than traditional SaaS. When AI agents take actions autonomously (booking, purchasing, executing), the vendor-customer relationship shifts toward "services" not "software." Liability allocation and oversight obligations change accordingly.

#### Data Licensing Agreements

**Essential provisions:**
- Permitted use: training, inference, testing, commercial deployment -- be specific
- Exclusivity: exclusive vs. non-exclusive license; field-of-use restrictions
- Geographic scope
- Sublicensing rights
- Quality and completeness warranties from licensor
- Audit rights: right to verify data is being used only as licensed
- Attribution requirements

**AI training data specifics:**
- Copyright status of training data: US Copyright Office (May 2025) report notes fair use defense for AI training is fact-specific and uncertain
- Indemnification for copyright infringement claims arising from licensed data
- Right to scrub or remove specific data points on request (right-to-deletion compliance)

#### AI/ML Service Agreements (custom development)

**Key negotiation points:**
- Who owns the model and weights upon project completion?
- Who owns improvements made during engagement?
- Can vendor reuse your data for other client projects?
- Deliverable acceptance criteria: how is model performance measured?
- Ongoing maintenance: what happens when model degrades?
- Exclusivity: is the model built only for you, or does vendor retain right to sell similar solutions?

#### Employment Contracts for AI Engineers

**Must-have provisions:**
- IP assignment: all inventions and work product created in scope of employment
- Prior inventions schedule: list what employee brings in (to avoid disputes)
- Confidentiality: trade secrets, model architectures, training data, prompts
- Non-solicitation: employees and customers (12-24 months post-termination)
- At-will vs. term employment: consider retention agreements for key technical staff
- Equity vesting: standard 4-year vest, 1-year cliff; acceleration on change of control

**AI-specific considerations:**
- Side projects clause: be explicit about what AI projects employee can do outside work
- Open source contribution policy: define what they can publish without approval
- Model and code contribution to OSS: ensure company retains commercial rights

#### SAFE Notes and Investment Documents

**SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) -- key negotiable terms:**
- **Valuation cap**: maximum valuation at which SAFE converts; post-money SAFE caps are now standard (YC model)
- **Discount rate**: 10-30% reduction on share price vs. next-round investors; rewards early risk
- **Pro-rata rights**: right to participate in next round to maintain ownership percentage
- **MFN clause** (Most Favored Nation): if better terms offered to later SAFE investors, you get upgraded too
- **Information rights**: quarterly financials, cap table access

**SAFE vs. Convertible Note:**
- SAFE: no maturity date, no interest accrual; simpler, founder-friendly
- Convertible note: has maturity date (creates pressure), accrues interest (increases dilution)
- 2025 data: SAFEs used in 90% of pre-seed deals on Carta; 64% of seed rounds

**Red flags for founders in term sheets:**
- Participating preferred stock (double-dip on liquidation)
- Full-ratchet anti-dilution (brutal for founders in down rounds)
- Cumulative dividends (compounds over time, reduces founder return)
- Super-voting preferred stock that overrides common at key decisions
- Blocking rights too broad (investor can veto ordinary business decisions)

#### Partnership Agreements

**Types:**
- Technology partnership: integration, co-development, mutual referrals
- Data partnership: data sharing for mutual AI development
- Channel partnership: reseller or white-label arrangement
- Strategic / commercial partnership: co-selling, co-marketing

**Key provisions:**
- Revenue sharing: clear formula, audit rights, payment timing
- IP ownership of jointly developed technology
- Exclusivity: carve-outs for existing customers and deals in progress
- Termination: who owns jointly developed assets after split?
- Non-compete: can each party work with the other's competitors?
- Change of control: does partnership survive acquisition?

### 4. Negotiation Frameworks

#### BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement)

**Definition**: Your best outcome if this negotiation fails entirely.

**For founders:**
- Know your BATNA before entering any negotiation
- The weaker your BATNA, the more leverage the other party has
- Strengthen BATNA by: having competing vendor options, running parallel negotiations, building in-house alternatives

**Practical application:**
- Vendor contract: BATNA = competitor's pricing + migration cost + delay cost
- Investment term sheet: BATNA = other investors, bootstrapping, revenue-based financing
- Employment offer: BATNA = other candidates, extending search, contracting option

#### ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement)

**Definition**: The range where both parties' acceptable outcomes overlap.

**Application:**
- Map out: your minimum acceptable terms, their minimum acceptable terms
- Identify which dimensions have ZOPA (price, scope, liability cap, term length)
- Trade across dimensions: give on price to get better IP terms; give on term length to get better data rights

**Multi-dimensional negotiation:**
- AI contracts have 5-10 key dimensions, each with its own ZOPA
- Prioritize: rank your terms by importance; concede on low-priority items to win high-priority ones
- Package deals: propose all terms together rather than negotiating each in isolation

#### Anchoring

**Definition**: The first number offered in a negotiation anchors subsequent discussion.

**Tactics:**
- Anchor high when you're the buyer offering a cap (liability cap, price cap)
- Anchor low when you're the seller quoting price or scope
- Respond to anchor with: "That's not in our range" + counter-anchor, not just a counter-number
- Research market rates to anchor credibly; unsupported anchors are easily dismissed

#### Principled Negotiation (Harvard Method)

- Separate people from the problem: don't make it personal
- Focus on interests, not positions: ask "why" behind demands
- Invent options for mutual gain: brainstorm before evaluating
- Insist on objective criteria: market rates, industry standards, legal norms

### 5. Red Flags in Contracts Founders Should Watch For

#### Data and IP Red Flags
- Vendor retains right to use your data for model training (without opt-in)
- Ambiguous output ownership: "outputs may be subject to third-party rights"
- No data deletion obligation on termination
- Vague IP assignment ("related to services") vs. explicit assignment

#### Liability and Risk Red Flags
- Liability capped at fees paid (inadequate for AI data breach or regulatory violations)
- Mutual indemnification that is actually one-sided in application
- Mandatory arbitration with expensive arbitration forum (AAA, JAMS) -- tilts against startup
- Class action waivers that prevent collective redress
- Governing law in unfavorable jurisdiction (especially for non-US entities)

#### Business Control Red Flags
- Unilateral right to modify terms (common in click-through agreements; negotiate enterprise terms)
- Automatic renewal with short opt-out window (< 30 days)
- Most-favored pricing clause that restricts your ability to give discounts to customers
- Change of control clause requiring vendor consent to your acquisition

#### Investment/Fundraising Red Flags
- Participating preferred with no cap (unlimited double-dip)
- Full-ratchet anti-dilution protection
- Overly broad information rights (operational interference)
- Veto rights on business decisions beyond major events (equity issuances, debt)
- Pay-to-play provisions that punish investors who don't follow on (affects your ability to bring in new investors)

#### Employment Red Flags
- Overbroad IP assignment ("any invention, whether or not related to company business")
- Non-compete in a ban state (unenforceable but creates chilling effect)
- No acceleration on change of control (leaves employees with unvested equity at acquisition)
- Ambiguous "cause" definition for termination (gives employer too much discretion)

### 6. When to Escalate to a Real Attorney

#### Always Use an Attorney For:
- **Incorporation and equity structure**: cap table mistakes are extremely costly to fix
- **First funding round**: SAFE or priced round with any investor
- **Material commercial contracts**: > $100K ARR, data access to your core systems, IP assignment
- **Employment agreements for co-founders and key executives**: equity vesting, IP assignment, change of control
- **Any regulatory matter**: data privacy enforcement, AI compliance, IP disputes
- **Acquisition discussions**: LOI, due diligence, definitive agreements

#### Use Attorney to Review (Not Just AI Tools):
- Any contract where you're the weaker party and have little leverage
- Contracts in regulated industries (healthcare, finance, legal) with AI components
- International contracts with governing law you don't understand
- Any indemnification clause that could expose you to unlimited liability

#### Can Use AI Tools + Self-Review For:
- Standard vendor agreements under $50K with mutual limitation of liability
- NDAs with established counterparties (use standard NVCA or Bonterms forms)
- Standard terms of service for consumer products (from reputable templates)
- Initial contract markup before attorney review

#### Signals That Require Immediate Attorney Involvement:
- Counterparty has sent you a demand letter or cease-and-desist
- You've discovered a potential IP ownership dispute in your company
- A regulatory agency has made contact (FTC, state AG, EU DPA)
- An investor is claiming you misrepresented something in fundraising materials
- You're about to sign anything that creates obligations > $500K or affects core IP

#### Finding AI-Knowledgeable Startup Counsel:
- Ask specifically: "Have you advised AI startups on data licensing and model IP?"
- Check: do they understand YC SAFE documents? Have they handled EU AI Act compliance?
- Avoid: general commercial attorneys without tech or startup experience
- Consider: firms that specialize in technology startups (Cooley, Wilson Sonsini, Gunderson, etc.)
